What you're looking for

A quick orientation, so the credentials below make sense at a glance.

The credential to look for

ATR-BC means a board-certified registered art therapist — the gold standard from the national Art Therapy Credentials Board. The best trauma art therapists pair it with a state counseling license like LPCC or LMFT.

Plus a trauma method that fits

On top of art therapy, strong trauma clinicians train in an evidence-based approach — EMDR, Anchored Relational Therapy, Brainspotting, Somatic Experiencing, or IFS. Every primary name here has at least one.

One Minnesota quirk

Minnesota doesn't license art therapists at the state level yet — a bill is pending. Until it passes, that ATR-BC + counseling-license combination is the quality signal. Good news: it's what this whole list is built on.

What good trauma art therapy feels like

So you know what to expect — and what a skilled clinician should and shouldn't do.

  1. Stability comes first. Early sessions focus on grounding and feeling safe — not diving straight into the hard memories.
  2. Materials are chosen with intention. Fluid media like watercolor or clay for release; firmer media like pencil for containment and control.
  3. Art is paired with a real trauma method. The making is woven together with an evidence-based approach like EMDR or Anchored Relational Therapy.
  4. It can be wordless when it needs to be. Art can move around the verbal-memory bottleneck that trauma often creates.
  5. It always moves at your pace. A good therapist never pushes past your consent or capacity. Ever.

Two quick things to keep in mind

Always confirm before you schedule

Waitlists and insurance networks shift often. A quick call to check current availability — and that your plan is in-network — saves a lot of hassle before a first appointment.

Why credentials matter extra here

Minnesota doesn't license art therapists yet, so there's no state board doing the vetting. The ATR-BC + counseling-license combo is the consumer's best quality signal — and it's the bar every primary name here clears.

Take your time with it.

This is a starting point, not medical advice — just a way to make the first call a little easier. Whatever you decide, and whenever, is the right pace.
